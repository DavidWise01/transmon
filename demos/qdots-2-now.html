How the screen uses them

In today's common design, blue LEDs excite red- and green-emitting dots to build pure white and full color.

setup blue backlight + dot color converters

so the panel gets bright, accurate color efficiently.

+1 next-gen "electroluminescent" QLEDs aim to drive each dot directly — no backlight — for even better screens.

In the clinic & beyond

Bioimaging tags

Dots are bright, color-tunable fluorescent labels that outlast traditional organic dyes.

edge brighter, far more photostable

so cells and molecules can be watched for longer.

+1 ordinary dyes "photobleach" — fade fast under the microscope; dots keep glowing, enabling long studies.

06

Lighting up tumors

Targeted dots can mark tumor tissue, helping surgeons see the edges of what to remove.

use image-guided surgery

so the boundary between cancer and healthy tissue gets clearer.

+1 near-infrared dots are favored here — that light penetrates tissue better and cuts background glow.

07

Many colors at once

Because each size is a distinct color, many dot types can be tracked simultaneously.

strength multiplexed imaging

so several targets can be watched in one experiment.

+1 one laser can excite them all at once, yet each size answers back in its own color — a built-in barcode.

08

Solar & sensing

Dots harvest light in experimental solar cells and detect it in photodetectors.

uses photovoltaics, photodetection

so they work as both light-emitters and light-catchers.

+1 "inverting" the dot — using it to absorb rather than emit — is exactly the photodetector idea.

The story so far
  • The flagship use is displays: dots' pure, tunable color drives many premium QLED TVs and monitors, plus high-quality LED lighting.
  • In today's common screens, a blue backlight excites red/green dots; next-gen designs aim to drive each dot directly.
  • In medicine, dots are bright, photostable fluorescent tags for bioimaging, multiplexed detection, and image-guided tumor surgery (often near-infrared).
  • They also serve in solar cells and photodetectors — working as light-catchers, not just light-emitters.
  • The whole arc — 1980s physics to mass-market product in decades — is why the 2023 Nobel celebrated it as basic science paying off.
How we know — and the honest caveats
  • Settled & deployed: QLED displays are a real, shipping product; dot bioimaging is established lab practice. These aren't promises.
  • Maturity varies: displays are mainstream; some medical and solar uses are still research or niche. "Used in medicine" spans both routine lab tags and experimental surgery — don't flatten the difference.
  • Toxicity is a live issue in the body: cadmium dots raise real safety questions for in vivo human use, which is why Cd-free dots and careful coatings matter — and why clinical adoption is cautious.
  • Marketing blurs lines: a "QLED" TV still uses an LCD panel with a dot layer; truly direct-emission QLED screens are mostly still emerging.
  • Book 1 was the chemistry; Book 3 is the frontier — dots as qubits, single-photon sources, and what's next.
